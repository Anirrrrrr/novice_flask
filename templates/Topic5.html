Modeling


Modeling is an important part of design, and is used much the same way architects use models. When an architect is designing a simple house or addition, he or she can handle the design work with a blueprint. For a complex house or office building, however, a model is usually built. The model has two purposes: 1) it's used for presentation - the architect uses the model to show and explain the details of the design; and 2) it's used for debugging. The three dimensional model shows problems that weren't obvious on the two dimensional blue print.
IT designers use modeling in the same way - for complex design - and for the same reasons - presentation and debugging. A data model, or a process model is a graphical representation of the design, and sometimes actually is a 3D graph. One of the major steps in data modeling is entity relationship (ER) diagramming. An ER model views the real world as a construct of entities and association between entities, and an ER chart shows, e.g., departments managing projects in a one-to-many (1 :N) relationship. The symbols used in the diagram supply the meanings connected with the entities and the relationship.
One of the current popular methodologies for process modeling is Use Case diagramming, where a software system is described based on how end-users will "use" the system, and is an important technique used in writing software requirements. Developers use a chart notation that is stick figures (the end-users, called actors) around a box that contains labeled ellipses (the actions). Then the modeler details the application processes using Use Case Scenarios describing how the software should work so that the technical team can turn the requirements into an application. Use-case notation is included in UML (Unified Modeling Language) and was introduced by Jacobson as part of OOSE (Object-Oriented System Engineering) in 1994. Modeling is being used more and more, and new modeling tools are being developed.
